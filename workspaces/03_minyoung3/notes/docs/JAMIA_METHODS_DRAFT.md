## Materials and Methods

### Study design

We developed ClaimTrap-AD as an evaluation framework for claim calibration in medical research agents. The framework asks an agent to convert structured biomedical analysis artifacts into a natural-language conclusion, then scores whether the conclusion stays within the strongest claim supported by the evidence. The study has two linked components: a 30-case pilot used to define the claim-ceiling task and evaluate open-ended agent outputs, and a scaled replication-grounded benchmark used to test statistical baselines and direct-question LLM agents at larger scale.

We treat an over-claim as a safety error: a statement that asserts predictive, transportable, causal, biomarker, or incremental value beyond what the artifact supports. We separately score completeness, because an output can be safe but uninformative. Claim ceilings are ordinal: L0 indicates no defensible claim; L1 indicates within-cohort association; L1.5 indicates within-cohort association with a mandatory negative-increment caveat; L2 indicates internal predictive validity; and L3 indicates transportable or deployable-biomarker evidence. The pilot uses the full ceiling scheme. The scaled benchmark uses replication-defined L0-L2 labels.

All inputs in this study are structured artifacts derived from tabular neuroimaging, clinical, and biomarker fields. The project inventory found no raw clinical free-text corpus; templated captions, when present, are derived from structured fields and were not used as clinical notes. No model training, preference optimization, or fine-tuning was performed for the reported evaluations.

### Data sources

The scaled benchmark was built from a canonical structured manifest containing 13,022 sessions from 7,231 subjects across seven cohorts: A4, ADNI, AIBL, AJU, KDRC, NACC, and OASIS. The scaled leave-one-cohort-out builder used six diagnostic cohorts (ADNI, NACC, AIBL, KDRC, OASIS, and AJU). A4 remained part of the source manifest but was excluded from diagnostic contrasts because it is a single-class amyloid-prevention cohort and therefore not suitable for the binary diagnostic endpoints used here.

The case builder selected one representative session per subject and required non-missing age, sex, and brain segmentation volume. Imaging features were FreeSurfer regional volumes represented as intracranial-volume-normalized volumes, raw volumes, and hemispheric asymmetry features. Five endpoints were specified (CN versus AD/dementia, CN versus MCI, MCI versus AD/dementia, CN versus dementia-only, and CN/MCI versus AD/dementia); four yielded cases meeting the per-cohort viability threshold, while CN versus dementia-only yielded no viable cohort and is absent from the released benchmark. Multivariate findings used predefined logistic-regression and random-forest model configurations over hippocampal, signature-region, or all intracranial-volume-normalized ROI features.

### Pilot benchmark and dual-view protocol

The pilot benchmark contains 30 locked cases: 10 OASIS-derived structured artifacts and 20 constructed probes. The constructed probes are controlled evaluation fixtures designed around observed evidence-to-claim failure modes, not claims of new biological findings. Cases span an E1-E8 taxonomy: covariate-baseline omission, incremental-value over-claim, temporal prediction over-claim, label-provenance over-claim, cross-cohort generalization over-claim, causal or mechanistic over-claim, negative-control shortcut misinterpretation, and unsupported biomarker claim. The locked set contains 4 E1, 4 E2, 4 E3, 4 E4, 4 E5, 3 E6, 5 E7, and 2 E8 cases.

Pilot gold labels were generated by two independent LLM reviewer agents, a critic persona and a domain-expert persona, that scored blinded artifacts without access to draft labels, intended taxonomy, revision notes, or answer-key fields. Consensus cases were mechanically locked, and tier-preserving disagreements were resolved by conservative tie-breaking. Disagreements crossing claim tiers or error families were escalated to manual adjudication; one case required this step. After that manual adjudication, all 30 labels were mechanically locked and marked scoring-allowed. Full human expert relabeling of the pilot set was not performed. Final pilot gold labels comprised 17 L1 cases, 10 L1.5 cases, and 3 L0 cases.

To avoid evaluation leakage, each case is represented by two views. The generation view contains only agent-visible inputs: task text, structured metrics, provenance, and a focus question. The scoring view contains judge-only fields: the gold claim level, allowed claim, forbidden assertions, required checks, primary error type, and adjudication metadata. Both generic and checklist prompts were scanned for gold, answer-key, and metadata leakage. In the locked 30-case run, the scan reported zero leaks across 60 prompts. The checklist prompt, implemented in code as the `verification_aware` arm, received only a global checklist and claim schema identical for every case; it did not receive case-specific gold.

### Pilot agents and Claim Safety Controller

The pilot compared three arms. The generic arm used a medical-research-assistant prompt and received only the generation-view artifact. The checklist arm used the same model and artifact but additionally received the global verifier checklist and ordinal claim schema. The Claim Safety Controller was evaluated separately as an inference-time rewrite controller, not as an independent open-ended generation arm.

The generic and checklist arms used `claude-sonnet-4-6` as the generation model under the same run configuration. For the n=3 recurrence run, decoding used temperature 1.0 and a 4,000-token output budget; the project default temperature 0 configuration was reserved for deterministic smoke and ablation runs. Each arm generated three outputs per case, giving 90 outputs per arm. The reported comparison is therefore a within-model prompt ablation rather than a model comparison.

The Claim Safety Controller implements a structured pipeline: generation view to candidate claim, deterministic evidence extraction, deterministic verifier modules, ceiling estimation, over-claim detection, constrained rewrite or blocking, and final enforcement. The LLM is used only to propose and rewrite text. Safety decisions are made by code over structured evidence and global verifier thresholds. The controller never reads scoring-view gold.

Controller verifier modules encode the E1-E8 evidence checks as deterministic functions. Examples include small or high-dimensional incremental AUROC gains without nested validation or paired confidence intervals, cross-sectional designs that cannot support temporal prediction, undocumented or pooled tracer labels, missing external validation for transportability, and site/vendor negative-control interpretations. The estimated ceiling is the strictest cap among triggered verifier modules, with L1 as the default when a within-cohort association remains supportable. Version 4 routes L0 cases to deterministic hard blocking and L1 or higher cases to semantic-preserving rewrite with strict post-rewrite enforcement.

For the controller experiment, the proposal was held fixed: the controller rewrote the generic n=1 draft and resampled the rewrite three times per case. The 90 controller outputs therefore measure rewrite stability on fixed proposals. They should not be interpreted as three independent controller generations matched to the generic and checklist arms.

### Pilot scoring and human review

Pilot outputs were scored by a reference-guided, non-self LLM judge that read the scoring view and the agent output. The reported n=3 pilot used GPT-5.5 as the judge for Sonnet-generated outputs. The judge prompt hid agent identity and scored each claim independently against a 0-3 rubric. Score 0 indicates an invalid or dangerous over-claim; score 1 indicates a partial response missing required limitations; score 2 indicates a calibrated within-ceiling claim; and score 3 indicates a stronger calibrated claim with additional required caveats. Hard-fail and over-claim indicators were recorded separately from completeness scores. The mean 0-3 score is therefore a calibrated-claim quality score that includes safety failures at the floor; it is not an independent verbosity or information-density metric.

The scoring protocol explicitly distinguishes calibrated negation from over-claiming. A statement such as "not a robust biomarker" is not counted as an over-claim merely because it contains a forbidden concept. Rule-based screens were used only as coarse gates or diagnostics; nuanced prose was judged against the gold reference. Earlier scorer diagnostics showed that calibrated negations can trigger keyword flags and should be reclassified as completeness gaps rather than safety failures when the prose remains within the claim ceiling. In the locked n=3 run, human-corrected recurrence review confirmed the persistent checklist failure on the E2 incremental-value case as a genuine safety over-claim in all three repeats. In a separate keyword-flag spot-check pack, all nine selected cases were confirmed as calibrated negations rather than safety over-claims (human over-claim rate 0/9), reclassifying their forbidden-keyword hits as completeness gaps.

### Scaled replication-grounded benchmark construction

The scaled benchmark was generated by `scripts/build_claimtrap_scaled.py`. For each endpoint, viable cohorts were required to contain at least 25 positive and 25 negative cases. For each case context, one cohort was assigned as the unseen gold cohort, one disjoint cohort as a held-out replication or tool cohort, and the remaining viable cohorts as the discovery set. This design separates the discovery signal, the replication-rule baseline signal, and the gold-label signal at the cohort level. The replication-rule baseline and the gold label are therefore not literal duplicates, although both are out-of-cohort replication estimates and should be interpreted as correlated signals.

Univariate cases evaluate individual ROI-derived features. Model cases evaluate predefined multivariate classifiers. Planted controls were added at graded signal strengths to provide known real and artifact anchors. For each case, the builder stores discovery AUROC, held-out tool-cohort replication AUROC, covariate-incremental AUROC when available, unseen gold-cohort AUROC, endpoint, finding type, gold label, ordinal ceiling, and difficulty stratum.

Gold labels were assigned from the unseen gold-cohort AUROC. For naturally occurring (non-planted) findings, a finding was labeled real when gold-cohort AUROC exceeded 0.60 and artifact otherwise. Planted controls instead retained their design label. In 11 of 2,888 cases (0.4%), all weak planted-real signals (8 in the hard-artifact stratum and 3 in the easy-artifact stratum), this design label remains real even though the weak effect did not exceed the gold-cohort replication threshold; we retain the design label intentionally, so that a replication rule rejecting a genuinely planted-but-weak real signal is counted as over-rejection rather than a correct call. This makes the hard stratum a more conservative test of replication-based calibration rather than a self-confirming one. Scaled ceilings were assigned as L0 when the finding did not replicate, L1 when gold-cohort AUROC was greater than 0.60 and at most 0.70, and L2 when it exceeded 0.70. The hard-artifact stratum was defined as high discovery-cohort AUROC with failed gold-cohort replication. A discovery-threshold rule is blind to this stratum by construction; this property is used to define the trap, not as an empirical claim that thresholding unexpectedly failed.

The E1-E8 pilot taxonomy informed construction of the LLM-reviewed pilot. It was not carried as a case-level label in the 2,888-case scaled JSONL. Scaled cases instead use replication-defined real/artifact labels, ordinal ceilings, and difficulty strata.

### Auto-gold reliability checks

We assessed auto-gold stability in three ways. For the 2,660 non-planted univariate and model cases with gold-cohort AUROC values, we tested whether real/artifact labels remained invariant when the real-label threshold varied across 0.55, 0.58, 0.60, 0.62, and 0.65; this analysis estimates threshold robustness and identifies the near-boundary region. For findings evaluated under at least two held-out gold cohorts, we measured unanimous cross-cohort real/artifact agreement. We also compared discovery, replication, and covariate-incremental signals between gold-real and gold-artifact groups as a convergent validity check.

A 120-case human-validation subset was exported from hard-artifact and planted cases. Human sign-off of this scaled subset was not complete at the time of the present draft, so the scaled labels are described as auto-gold labels with reliability checks rather than as labels with completed human sign-off.

### Statistical baselines on the scaled benchmark

Two deterministic baselines were evaluated for artifact detection. The discovery-threshold rule predicts a finding as real when discovery AUROC is at least 0.60 and artifact otherwise. The replication-rule baseline predicts artifact when the held-out tool-cohort replication AUROC is missing or at most 0.58, or when the covariate-incremental AUROC is available and at most 0.02; otherwise it predicts real. This replication-rule baseline is a statistical baseline on the scaled benchmark and is distinct from the pilot Claim Safety Controller.

Metrics were computed for artifact detection: recall, precision, F1 score, and accuracy. Results were reported overall and by prespecified subsets: all cases, the hard-artifact stratum, non-planted univariate/model cases, and planted controls.

### Direct-question LLM evaluation on the scaled benchmark

We evaluated open LLMs as direct-question subjects on a stratified sample of the scaled benchmark using `scripts/run_agent_eval_scaled.py`. The script sampled up to 120 cases per difficulty stratum with a fixed random seed, yielding 480 cases. Each model was evaluated under two prompts: a generic direct-question prompt and a checklist prompt that instructed the model to require independent replication and covariate-incremental value before answering that a finding generalized. The input text showed only discovery-side information and explicitly stated that no external-cohort result was given.

The evaluated models were Qwen3-32B and MedGemma-27B. For Qwen3-32B, thinking was disabled through the chat-template configuration. Decoding used the local LLM provider, temperature 0, and a short JSON-only output budget. Responses were parsed into "yes", "no", or "uncertain" stances. In this arm, over-claiming was defined narrowly as answering "yes" to generalization on an artifact-labeled case. No LLM judge or prose-level over-claim scoring was used for the scaled direct-question arm, so its results should be interpreted as direct stance behavior rather than open-ended claim-writing behavior.

### Statistical reporting

The pilot is reported descriptively as a controlled benchmark pilot, not as a powered superiority trial. We report counts of over-claims and hard-fails, mean 0-3 completeness, per-arm output counts, and recurrence patterns. We do not perform hypothesis testing for the pilot arms.

The scaled benchmark reports deterministic classification metrics and label-reliability summaries. The discovery-threshold rule's hard-artifact 0% recall is reported as a definitional consequence of the stratum and rule, not as an empirical headline. The empirical emphasis is the residual difficulty after adding disjoint replication information: the fraction of hard natural traps recovered by the replication-rule baseline and the fraction left unresolved.

### Reproducibility and scope

Deterministic components include the scaled case builder, statistical baselines, structured evidence extraction, verifier modules, ceiling estimation, over-claim detection, and post-rewrite enforcement. LLM generation and judging rely on closed or local model runtimes and are reproducible only conditional on the recorded model identifiers, prompts, decoding settings, and saved raw outputs. The reported artifacts include benchmark JSONL files, prompt manifests, leakage scans, controller traces, judge prompts, judge verdicts, token usage, and summary reports.

Gold provenance is a central scope limitation. The pilot uses LLM reviewer-agent labels with one manually adjudicated disagreement, and the scaled benchmark uses auto-gold replication labels with reliability checks. Neither component currently has complete independent human expert gold labels.

This study evaluates claim calibration over structured neuroimaging research artifacts. It does not evaluate clinical deployment, diagnostic decision-making, arbitrary biomedical free text, or model safety in unconstrained settings.

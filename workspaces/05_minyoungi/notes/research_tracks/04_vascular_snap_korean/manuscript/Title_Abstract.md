## Abstract (structured; ~250 words)

**Background.** Cerebrovascular white matter hyperintensities (WMH) are implicated in hippocampal neurodegeneration, yet prior work localized the WMH–hippocampus association to amyloid-positive individuals, and most studies graded WMH with coarse visual scales that may attenuate a true association below detectability. Whether automated quantification recovers a WMH–hippocampus association that visual grading misses—specifically in the amyloid-negative stratum, and in an East Asian population—is unknown.

**Methods.** We performed a cross-sectional analysis of amyloid-negative older adults from a Korean memory clinic (n = 643). WMH were quantified on fluid-attenuated inversion recovery imaging with a contrast-agnostic deep-learning model, and hippocampal volume was derived from T1-weighted imaging with a deep-learning segmentation pipeline; both were normalized to intracranial volume. Linear regression related WMH burden (z-scored log-volume) to normalized hippocampal volume, adjusting for age, sex, apolipoprotein E ε4 count, and education, with a robustness battery including a non-circular global-atrophy control derived from a different image and tool, a 32-specification analysis, and mediation analyses. The same model was refit with the ordinal visual grade for comparison, and positive controls and a native-versus-registered analysis assessed validity and registration confounding.

**Results.** Greater WMH burden was associated with smaller hippocampal volume with quantitative segmentation (standardized β = −0.17, 95% confidence interval −0.22 to −0.12) but not with the three-level visual grade in the same participants (β = −0.07, P = 0.08)—an attenuation consistent with measurement error. The quantitative association persisted after the non-circular global-atrophy adjustment (β = −0.12) and was negative across all 32 specifications, indicating a WMH-specific signal independent of global atrophy. Hypertension and diabetes predicted greater WMH, yet vascular risk factors showed no net association with hippocampal volume, with WMH on the mediating path. The association was unchanged after adjustment for registration quality.

**Conclusions.** In amyloid-negative older Korean adults, quantitative WMH burden was associated with smaller hippocampal volume independent of global atrophy—a vascular signature undetected by conventional visual grading. Automated WMH quantification may identify a vascular signature associated with smaller hippocampal volume that visual rating overlooks.

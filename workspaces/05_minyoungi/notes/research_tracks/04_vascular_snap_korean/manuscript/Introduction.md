# Introduction

Cerebral small vessel disease, of which white matter hyperintensities (WMH) on fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) are the most common marker, is highly prevalent in older adults and contributes to cognitive decline and dementia.¹ The hippocampus is central to episodic memory, and hippocampal atrophy is a core imaging substrate of cognitive impairment across the Alzheimer's disease continuum and beyond it.² Understanding which factors drive hippocampal neurodegeneration—particularly in individuals without amyloid pathology—has direct implications for risk stratification and for the design of preventive interventions targeting modifiable vascular pathways.

Within the amyloid/neurodegeneration biomarker framework, a substantial proportion of older adults exhibit neurodegeneration in the absence of amyloid positivity, a profile for which cerebrovascular disease is a leading candidate contributor.³ Consistent with this, greater WMH burden has been associated with smaller hippocampal volume and with hippocampal atrophy over time.⁴ ⁵ However, the relationship is not simple: some studies report that WMH affects hippocampal volume specifically in amyloid-positive individuals,⁶ others find amyloid and WMH to act additively without interaction,⁷ and others find total WMH unrelated to hippocampal volume except in apolipoprotein E (APOE) ε4 carriers.⁸ Whether, and in whom, WMH is associated with hippocampal volume therefore remains unsettled.

Two features of the existing evidence limit its interpretation. First, most studies quantified WMH with coarse ordinal visual scales, which compress the upper range of burden and have limited reliability; an association that is real but modest may be attenuated below detectability by such measurement error. Second, the evidence derives predominantly from Western cohorts, leaving the generalizability of any amyloid-stratified WMH–hippocampus relationship to East Asian populations—who carry a high cerebrovascular burden—largely untested. Automated, contrast- and resolution-agnostic deep-learning segmentation now enables reproducible quantitative WMH measurement in heterogeneous clinical scans,⁹ creating an opportunity to test whether a genuine association has been missed for want of measurement granularity.

However, no study has combined these elements to ask whether quantitative WMH burden is associated with hippocampal volume specifically in amyloid-negative individuals, whether such an association is independent of global brain atrophy rather than a reflection of generalized neurodegeneration, and whether conventional visual grading is adequate to detect it. Resolving these questions requires a cohort with deep-learning WMH quantification, amyloid stratification, hippocampal volumetry, and vascular risk-factor data in a population in which the question has not been examined.

We therefore examined, in amyloid-negative Korean older adults from a memory clinic, the cross-sectional association of automated deep-learning WMH burden with hippocampal volume. We tested whether the association was specific to WMH and independent of global atrophy, whether it was detectable with conventional visual grading, and how it related to clinical vascular risk factors.

# Discussion

In amyloid-negative older adults from a Korean memory clinic, greater quantitative white matter hyperintensity (WMH) burden was associated with smaller hippocampal volume. The association was independent of nonlinear age and of a non-circular global-atrophy control, was stable across 32 model specifications, and persisted after adjustment for vascular risk factors and for image-registration quality. Notably, the same association was not detectable with the conventional three-level visual grade, and the magnitude of the visual-grade attenuation matched what measurement-error theory predicts. Vascular risk factors predicted greater WMH and lay upstream of it, but showed no net association with hippocampal volume.

Our central observation differs in an important way from the most directly comparable prior report. Freeze and colleagues found that WMH was associated with reduced hippocampal volume in amyloid-positive, but not amyloid-negative, individuals,⁶ the opposite stratum to our finding. Two non-exclusive explanations are plausible and testable. First, in amyloid-positive individuals the hippocampus may already be substantially reduced by Alzheimer-type tau pathology, leaving little additional variance for a vascular contribution to explain (a floor effect), whereas in amyloid-negative individuals WMH-related variation in hippocampal volume is unmasked. Second, the cohorts differ in size, WMH measurement (semi-quantitative visual versus automated quantitative), and amyloid ascertainment (cerebrospinal fluid versus visual read), any of which could shift the apparent locus of the association; our own finding that visual grading failed to detect the association in the same participants illustrates how measurement choice alone can change conclusions. Our results are broadly consistent with reports that WMH and amyloid contribute to hippocampal measures in a largely independent, additive fashion,⁷ and they extend a Korean line of work that has examined amyloid and WMH in the reverse direction.[VERIFY — KBASE, PMID 38454444] They contrast with the report that the WMH–hippocampus association is confined to APOE ε4 carriers,⁸ as our association was present in both ε4 carriers and non-carriers.

Mechanistically, the pattern we observed—vascular risk factors predicting WMH, WMH associated with hippocampal volume, and a significant indirect path from vascular risk through WMH to the hippocampus—is consistent with a model in which systemic vascular risk produces white-matter injury that is, in turn, accompanied by hippocampal volume loss. Candidate substrates linking small vessel disease to hippocampal vulnerability include chronic hypoperfusion, blood–brain-barrier dysfunction, and disruption of limbic white-matter tracts.[VERIFY — mechanistic references] Because the analysis is cross-sectional, these interpretations are associative; we make no causal claim and note that reverse or shared-pathway explanations cannot be excluded.

Two findings were not anticipated and merit cautious interpretation. First, the association was undetectable with visual grading yet robust with quantitative segmentation; rather than a discrepancy, this is the expected consequence of attenuation by a coarse, saturating three-level scale, as supported by the observed ratio of standardized coefficients. The practical implication is that automated quantification recovered this association where visual reading did not, illustrating how measurement granularity can determine detectability. Second, vascular risk factors themselves were not associated with hippocampal volume despite predicting WMH; the brain-level WMH, not clinical risk-factor status, was the marker associated with the hippocampus. We interpret this as evidence that the proximal imaging phenotype carries information that distal risk-factor status does not, although the small, suppression-type mediation pattern (a significant indirect path with a null total effect) should be read descriptively rather than as a clean causal decomposition.

This study has several strengths. We used reproducible deep-learning WMH quantification rather than visual grading, in a population under-represented in this literature. The amyloid-negative association survived a deliberately conservative, non-circular global-atrophy control, a 32-specification multiverse, and a registration-confound analysis combining native-versus-registered comparison with adjustment for registration quality. Complete vascular risk-factor data allowed a positive control confirming that the automated WMH measure captured vascular pathology.

Several limitations temper these strengths. First, the design is cross-sectional; temporal and causal inferences are not warranted, and a longitudinal study is needed to establish whether WMH precedes hippocampal volume loss. Second, the primary finding rests on a single well-powered cohort; the second cohort was small in its amyloid-negative subgroup, did not retain specificity after global-atrophy adjustment, and showed a non-specific pattern, so we report it as a directionally consistent but underpowered observation rather than as replication. Third, the WMH and hippocampal volumes were normalized by intracranial-volume estimates from two different tools, and native thick-slice FLAIR yielded systematically higher WMH volumes than the registered pipeline; the within-stratum standardized analyses are invariant to these scale differences, but absolute volumes should not be compared across pipelines. Fourth, our finding is the opposite stratum to a prior report and should be regarded as hypothesis-generating until tested in independent quantitative-WMH, amyloid-stratified cohorts. We mitigated these concerns through extensive sensitivity analyses and transparent reporting of the weaker cohort, and the question we address—who is at risk of hippocampal neurodegeneration in the absence of amyloid—remains clinically important regardless.

In amyloid-negative older Korean adults, about a one-fifth-of-a-standard-deviation smaller hippocampal volume accompanied each standard-deviation increase in quantitative WMH burden, independent of global atrophy and undetected by visual grading. These findings suggest that automated WMH quantification can identify a vascular signature associated with smaller hippocampal volume in amyloid-negative individuals that conventional visual rating overlooks, and support incorporating quantitative WMH into the assessment of memory-clinic patients without amyloid pathology.
